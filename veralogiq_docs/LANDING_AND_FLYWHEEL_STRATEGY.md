# Landing Page & Flywheel Strategy
## Game: My Pokémon Collection · Primary user: Player/Student · Goal: Word of mouth + lead gen

---

## 1. Primary user and job-to-be-done

- **Primary user:** The player/student (kid or any age) who will play the game.
- **Job-to-be-done:** “I want to play a fun Pokémon-style game that also feels okay for learning / screen time.”
- **Landing page job:** Answer “What is this?” and get them to **Play** with minimal friction. No signup, no paywall — one tap to play.

Secondary users (lead gen): **Parents** (tutoring) and **Teachers** (classroom use). They discover the game when the player does, or when another parent/teacher shares it. They are not the main audience of the landing page hero; they are a clear “second path” so the page doesn’t feel like an ad.

---

## 2. Word-of-mouth flywheel

A **flywheel** is a loop where each turn makes the next turn easier. Here, the loop is:

```
[1] Great play experience
    (fun, fair difficulty, “I caught one!”, “I finished Trivia!”)
           ↓
[2] Players share + parents/teachers notice
    (kid tells friend “play this”; parent sees Veralogiq; teacher tries it in class)
           ↓
[3] More players + more leads
    (more kids play; more parents/teachers hit For Families / For Teachers)
           ↓
[4] Revenue + proof
    (tutoring signups; classroom use; testimonials / “used in 10 classrooms”)
           ↓
[5] Better product + more games
    (invest in more games, polish, maybe “Starter set” or new modes)
           ↓
    back to [1] Great play experience
```

**How word of mouth actually happens**

- **Player → player:** “There’s this game where you catch Pokémon by doing math and trivia” — share link (same URL as landing/game). No account needed, so the link is enough.
- **Player → parent:** Parent sees kid playing, sees “Veralogiq” and “Learning games & tutoring” — curiosity. Landing or footer sends them to **For Families** (tutoring).
- **Teacher → teacher:** Teacher uses game in class, gets permission letter, tells colleague “free, no signup, I got a letter from Veralogiq.” Link + **For Teachers** page.
- **Parent → parent:** “My kid actually does the math to get more Poké Balls” — share link. Other parent clicks, kid plays, that parent may later look at tutoring.

**What makes the flywheel spin faster**

- **No signup to play** — share = send link. Friction stays low.
- **One clear URL** — game and landing can be same page (scroll or section) or one click to play.
- **Memorable moments** — “I caught 50,” “I finished the Trivia,” “Relaxed vs Challenge mode.” These are natural things to say when sharing.
- **Trust for grown-ups** — “Classroom permission,” “Bay Area,” “We design for different ways to learn” (ability-aware) so parents/teachers feel good recommending it.
- **Lead gen that doesn’t block play** — Players never have to give email to play. Leads come from parents/teachers who choose to click For Families / For Teachers or contact on Instagram.

## 3. Landing page strategy

**Role of the landing page**

- First touch for anyone who gets a link (shared by friend, teacher, parent, or found via search/social).
- Primary conversion: **Start playing** (player).
- Secondary: **I’m a parent** or **I’m a teacher** → go to existing Parents / Teachers pages (lead gen).

**Audience order**

1. **Player/student** — hero and main CTA.
2. **Parent** — one short block or line + link to For Families (tutoring).
3. **Teacher** — one short block or line + link to For Teachers (classroom, permission).

**Suggested structure (concept only)**

- **Hero**
  - Headline: Speaks to the player. Examples: “Catch Pokémon. Earn them with math and trivia.” or “Learn a little. Catch a lot.”
  - Subline: One sentence — e.g. “Free game, no signup. Play in your browser.”
  - Single primary CTA: **Play the game** (same as current “play” entry: e.g. index or game start).
- **One short “What is this?”**
  - 2–3 sentences: You catch Gen 1 Pokémon by earning Poké Balls (Math Challenge or Trivia), then answering a quick question to catch. Relaxed and Challenge modes so different players can enjoy it. Made by Veralogiq — learning games and tutoring.
- **Social proof (when you have it)**
  - Optional line: “Used in classrooms,” “Loved by kids who hate worksheets,” or a short quote. Add when available; don’t invent.
- **Two small “Also for you” blocks**
  - **For parents:** “Want tutoring in math, coding, or robotics? Same team that built this game.” → Link to For Families (and Instagram).
  - **For teachers:** “Free to use in class. We grant permission — no lengthy forms.” → Link to For Teachers.
- **Footer**
  - Same as current: About, Settings, For Teachers, For Students, For Families, Veralogiq.

**What to avoid**

- Don’t make the landing page feel like an ad for tutoring. Tutoring is a clear second path, not the hero.
- Don’t add signup or email gate to play. Word of mouth relies on “click link → play.”
- Don’t overload the hero. One headline, one subline, one Play CTA.

**Where the landing “lives”**

- Option A: **Same URL as the game** — e.g. veralogiq.com/game or root. First screen is this hero + Play; scroll or second screen is “What is this?” and For Parents / For Teachers. After “Play,” they’re in the current game (index).
- Option B: **Separate landing URL** — e.g. veralogiq.com/play or /pokemon. Landing has hero + CTA “Play” → goes to game (index). Game stays as is.
- Option C: **Game is the landing** — current index.html gets a compact “above the fold” hero (title + tagline + one “How I play” + Play) so first-time visitors see a clear invite before the full UI. More design work.

Recommendation: Start with **Option B** (dedicated landing page, one URL for “share this game”) so the game itself doesn’t have to change. Landing’s only job is explain + Play + soft paths to Parents/Teachers.

---

## 4. Lead generation (without breaking the flywheel)

- **Who are the leads:** Parents (tutoring), teachers (classroom use, permission letter, later maybe paid classroom packs).
- **How they enter:** They discover the game via the player (their kid or student), then click **For Families** or **For Teachers** from the game footer, brand bar, or landing page.
- **What we don’t do:** No email capture to play, no pop-ups before play, no “Share to unlock.” Play stays free and open; leads are optional next steps for grown-ups.
- **Where we capture intent:** For Families and For Teachers pages already send them to Instagram (and any future form). Landing page just routes them there with clear, short copy.
- **Optional later:** “Get a reminder” or “Get permission letter” with optional email on For Teachers — only if you want a list; not required for the flywheel.

---

## 5. Metrics that matter (for the flywheel)

- **Play:** Unique visits to game (or “Play” from landing), sessions, return visits. (Shows whether the experience is good enough to share.)
- **Sharing:** Referrers (where traffic comes from), or “Share this game” clicks if you add a button. (Proxy for word of mouth.)
- **Leads:** Instagram DMs from For Families / For Teachers, or form submits if you add them. (Conversion from player → parent/teacher interest.)
- **Revenue:** Tutoring signups, sessions. (Enables “better product + more games.”)

You don’t need all of these on day one. Start with: **Can people find the game from a link? Do they click Play? Do any parents/teachers click through to For Families / For Teachers?**

---

## 6. One-paragraph summary

**Landing:** One page aimed first at the player: headline + one sentence + “Play the game.” Below: short “What is this?”, then two small blocks — For parents (tutoring), For teachers (classroom) — with links to existing pages. No signup to play.

**Flywheel:** Great play experience → players share and parents/teachers notice → more players and more leads → tutoring revenue and proof → better product and more games → better experience. Word of mouth is the main growth lever; the landing page and game are the shareable asset; lead gen lives on For Families and For Teachers without blocking play.
